How to Build Scalable Video Conferencing Tools in the New Remote World

If 2020 has a defining technology, it is without a doubt the video conference. What was once a tool for occasional remote meetings has become the central nervous system for businesses, schools, and even families. It is the digital room where work happens, where doctors meet patients, and where strategy is set.

As we discussed in our post on "Digital Transformation Post-COVID," this shift is permanent. The demand for reliable, high-quality video is not a temporary spike; it is the new baseline. But as many companies discovered, scaling video is notoriously difficult. The early-year struggles of even established platforms highlighted the immense architectural challenges involved.

Simply "adding video" to an application is not enough. You must design for scale, security, and performance from day one. Here’s how.

The Foundation: Understanding WebRTC

At the heart of modern video communication on the internet is WebRTC (Web Real-Time Communication). WebRTC is an open-source project, originally from Google, that provides browsers and mobile applications with the ability to communicate directly with each other via video, audio, and data without needing complex plugins or native software. It's the engine behind many of the platforms you use every day.

But while WebRTC handles the peer-to-peer connection, it doesn't solve the problem of scale. How do you connect 10, 50, or 500 people in a single call efficiently? This requires a server-side architecture.

The Critical Choice: SFU vs. MCU Architecture

When you have a multi-party call, you can't just connect everyone to everyone else in a "mesh" network. The processing power and bandwidth required on each user's device would quickly become overwhelming. The solution is a central server that manages the media streams. There are two main approaches:

  • Multipoint Control Unit (MCU): An MCU is a media server that acts like a mixing board. It receives the video and audio streams from every participant, decodes them, combines them into a single new stream, and sends that composite stream back to every participant.
    • Pro: Less demanding on the end-user's device.
    • Con: Extremely CPU-intensive on the server. Scaling is expensive and complex.
  • Selective Forwarding Unit (SFU): An SFU is a smarter, more efficient media router. It receives an individual stream from each participant but doesn't mix them. Instead, it forwards each incoming stream to all other participants. Each user's device receives multiple individual streams and handles rendering them.
    • Pro: Far less CPU-intensive on the server, making it much easier and cheaper to scale. It also offers greater layout flexibility on the client-side.
    • Con: Requires more download bandwidth on the client-side (though this is manageable).

As a 2018 whitepaper from the WebRTC experts at webrtcHacks details, the SFU model has become the de-facto standard for building large-scale, cost-effective video applications. It is the architecture that powers most of the modern platforms that have successfully scaled to meet 2020's unprecedented demand.

Essential Design Pillars for a World-Class Video App

Beyond the core SFU architecture, building a competitive platform requires focusing on these key areas:

  1. Cloud-Native Elasticity: Your SFU servers must run on a cloud infrastructure like AWS or Azure. This allows you to automatically spin up new media servers in different regions as demand grows and shrink the footprint during off-hours, ensuring both performance and cost-efficiency.
  2. Ironclad Security: The "Zoombombing" incidents of early 2020 were a harsh lesson in the importance of security. Your platform must include:
    • End-to-End Encryption (E2EE): To ensure no one, not even the server, can intercept the content of a call.
    • Secure Signaling: The process of setting up the call must be authenticated and encrypted.
    • Robust Access Controls: Features like waiting rooms, password-protected meetings, and strong host controls are no longer optional.
  3. Adaptive Bitrate Streaming (ABR): Network conditions are unpredictable. A robust platform must dynamically adjust the quality of the video stream up or down based on the user's available bandwidth. This ensures the call remains stable and usable, even on a weak connection.
